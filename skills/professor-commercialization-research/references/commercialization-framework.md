# Commercialization Framework

Use this framework to turn academic research into realistic business options.

## Commercialization lenses

Assess each research direction through these lenses:

- **Pain intensity**: Is the problem costly, recurring, regulated, or strategically urgent?
- **Buyer clarity**: Is there a named buyer such as CISO, data platform head, compliance lead, CTO, exchange risk head, hospital data lead, or AI platform owner?
- **Repeatability**: Can the solution be sold many times with limited customization?
- **Integration burden**: Does the product require deep access to sensitive systems, long deployment, or bespoke engineering?
- **Data moat**: Does performance improve with proprietary labels, benchmarks, traces, evaluation cases, or customer feedback?
- **Trust requirement**: Would customers prefer a neutral third party, audit report, certification, or university-backed credibility?
- **Regulatory tailwind**: Are laws, standards, audits, or procurement policies pushing adoption?
- **Build-vs-buy risk**: Which parts will large customers self-build, and which parts are too specialized or neutral to self-build?
- **Evidence maturity**: Is the paper a prototype, measured system, deployed system, or theoretical method?

## Product forms

Use precise product language:

- **Core component**: SDK, engine, optimizer, protocol library, privacy-preserving join, evaluator, or scanner.
- **Platform module**: dashboard, workflow, policy management, monitoring, or orchestration around the core component.
- **Data asset**: labeled risk database, benchmark suite, threat intelligence, or model/prompt evaluation corpus.
- **Assessment service**: audit, certification, red-team test, privacy risk review, or deployment readiness report.
- **Custom project**: high-touch integration or consulting; useful for revenue but weaker as venture-scale product.

## Build-vs-buy analysis

Expect strategic customers to self-build:

- Business data ownership
- Internal workflow portals
- Final policy decisions
- Deployment into sensitive infrastructure
- Domain-specific rules that encode proprietary operations

Expect customers to buy or co-develop:

- Hard cryptographic/privacy-preserving components
- Continuously updated risk labels, attack corpora, and benchmarks
- Neutral third-party audits and compliance evidence
- Specialized profilers, validators, or verification tools
- Cross-organization trust infrastructure

## Commercial benchmark scan

For each direction, list:

- Foreign commercial companies with a product or paid service in the category
- Domestic commercial companies with a product or paid service in the category
- Adjacent large-platform features that may absorb the category
- Open-source projects or infrastructure substitutes
- Benchmark directness: direct product, adjacent product, platform feature, open source, service-only, or temporarily blank
- Product white space or blank reason
- Why a new company could still win

If no clear commercial company exists, say "temporarily blank/sparse" and explain whether the reason is early market, weak demand, academic-only technology, consulting-only delivery, or a category hidden inside larger platforms. Do not list research papers, generic cloud vendors, or open-source libraries as "companies" unless they are explicitly labeled as adjacent substitutes.

## Recommendation format

For each business option, write:

1. One-sentence product thesis
2. Target customer and budget owner
3. Research basis
4. What the first product actually does
5. Why customers would buy instead of build
6. Domestic and international commercial-company benchmarks or white-space judgment
7. Main adoption blocker
8. 3-6 month validation plan
9. Go / watch / avoid judgment

Prefer one lead wedge plus secondary options. Avoid presenting every paper direction as equally investable.
