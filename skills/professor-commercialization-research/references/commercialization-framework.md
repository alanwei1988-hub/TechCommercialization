# Commercialization Framework 2.0

Use this framework to turn academic capabilities into a ranked commercialization decision. Do not stop at `有应用前景`. Decide what could be sold, who would buy, why they would switch, what vehicle should carry the work, what could invalidate the thesis, and what to test next.

## 1. Evidence posture

Separate four evidence classes:

- **Technical Evidence**: what papers, prototypes, datasets, patents, code, or measured systems directly demonstrate.
- **Market Evidence**: current products, vendors, procurement, pricing, regulation, standards, customer workflows, or credible industry sources.
- **Inference**: a reasoned bridge from evidence to a product or market hypothesis.
- **Interview Needed**: a fact that only the scholar, lab, technology-transfer office, customer, partner, or regulator can establish.

Record both supporting and challenging evidence. Do not treat publication volume, citation count, technical novelty, or market size alone as proof of commercial attractiveness.

## 2. Opportunity scoring

Score no more than three opportunities on a 0-5 scale for each dimension. Calculate the weighted total as `score / 5 * weight`.

| Dimension | Weight | What to assess |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Demand and buyer clarity | 25 | Pain intensity, frequency, economic consequence, named user/buyer/budget owner, purchase trigger, and urgency. |
| Productization readiness | 20 | Evidence maturity, robustness, data, hardware, workflow, validation, deployment, manufacturing, regulatory, and delivery gaps. |
| Differentiation and defensibility | 20 | Unique capability, data/know-how/IP moat, trust or neutrality, benchmark advantage, and platform/substitute risk. |
| Adoption and commercial access | 15 | Integration burden, procurement path, workflow change, sales cycle, channel access, and availability of a beachhead customer. |
| Economics and repeatability | 10 | Pilot offer, pricing unit, delivery cost, gross-margin logic, customization burden, and ability to sell repeatedly. |
| Transfer and team readiness | 10 | IP ownership, school-transfer path, PI/student commitment, operating owner, engineering capacity, and governance. |

Use scores as a decision aid, not false precision. For every dimension, add one sentence of evidence and one sentence on missing proof.

## 3. Hard blockers and judgments

Check hard blockers outside the score:

- The product claim is contradicted by the actual technical evidence.
- No identifiable buyer, budget owner, or purchase reason remains after checking current solutions.
- IP, data rights, open-source licensing, regulatory, safety, or school-transfer constraints make the proposed route infeasible.
- A platform, free substitute, or customer self-build path absorbs the full value with no defensible third-party role.
- The required performance, manufacturing, validation, or integration step is not realistically testable with available resources.
- The only viable delivery is non-repeatable custom research while the thesis assumes a scalable product company.

Assign judgments:

- **Go**: score at least 70, no confirmed hard blocker, evidence confidence is at least Medium, and a concrete 90-day validation path exists.
- **Watch**: score 50-69; or score at least 70 with Low confidence, an unresolved potential blocker, or no accessible validation customer.
- **Avoid**: score below 50 or a confirmed hard blocker invalidates the proposed route.

If no opportunity qualifies, say so. Do not force a Lead Wedge for presentation symmetry.

Assess confidence separately:

- **High**: direct technical evidence plus current market evidence and at least one direct deployment, customer, procurement, or interview signal.
- **Medium**: strong technical evidence plus multiple credible current market sources, but no direct customer validation.
- **Low**: mostly inference, adjacent analogies, sparse market sources, uncertain identity/IP, or unverified product claims.

## 4. Customer and current-solution analysis

For every opportunity, distinguish:

- Core user.
- Economic buyer.
- Budget owner and procurement owner.
- Job-to-be-done and workflow entry point.
- Pain frequency, cost, risk, quality loss, delay, or missed revenue.
- Purchase or switching trigger.
- Current solution and why it is tolerated.

Check all relevant current-solution categories before claiming whitespace:

- Customer self-build.
- Manual work, Excel, rules, or expert judgment.
- Traditional supplier, legacy software, instrument, device, or process vendor.
- Open source or a large platform's built-in feature.
- Consulting, testing, certification, outsourcing, or system integration.
- Joint development with a university or institute.
- No systematic solution, delayed handling, or post-incident remediation.

Capture what each alternative costs in money, time, risk, quality, opportunity, or internal headcount. State the dissatisfaction, switching barrier, and likely procurement route.

## 5. Product and economics

Choose a concrete product form:

- SaaS / Web product.
- SDK / API / engine.
- Hardware, instrument, device, or core module.
- Materials or process package.
- Data asset, benchmark, or labeled dataset.
- Assessment, testing, audit, or certification service.
- Vertical workflow tool or industry solution.
- Consulting or custom project.
- Licensing or IP transfer.

For the Lead Wedge, define:

- First deliverable or MVP.
- Core workflow and must-have features.
- Data, hardware, deployment, manufacturing, validation, or regulatory requirements.
- Success metric expressed in customer terms.
- What V1 explicitly excludes.
- Productization gap between paper evidence and a sellable product.

State an early economics hypothesis when evidence permits:

- Pilot offer and what it proves.
- Pricing unit: project, seat, API call, device, sample, license, subscription, report, throughput, or outcome.
- Plausible price range and evidence source.
- Delivery effort and customization burden.
- Likely sales/procurement cycle.
- Repeatability and gross-margin logic.

Write `需要验证` instead of inventing price, market size, conversion, or willingness to pay. Prefer bottom-up beachhead sizing over generic top-down TAM claims.

## 6. Commercialization vehicle

Do not assume company formation is the default. Compare:

- Startup or spinout.
- Licensing / IP transfer.
- Joint development with an established company.
- Research service or testing lab.
- Instrument or core-module supply.
- Industry consortium, standards, audit, or certification service.
- Open source plus enterprise support.

Assess each route by IP fit, operating commitment, capital need, speed to first revenue/evidence, repeatability, regulatory burden, customer trust, and school-governance complexity.

If a startup is recommended, state:

- Company label and one-line positioning.
- First beachhead market.
- Business model.
- Expansion path.
- Why a new company can win despite platforms, incumbents, open source, and self-build.

Provide customer, investor, and university/technology-transfer versions of positioning only when they materially differ.

## 7. Team and transfer readiness

Define the minimum team required to validate the Lead Wedge:

- Scientist / PI.
- Operating CEO or business lead.
- Product owner.
- Core engineering lead.
- Industry BD / sales.
- Delivery / solutions.
- Special roles for regulatory, quality, manufacturing, IP, security, clinical affairs, metrology, or standards.

State which roles can be part-time in the first 90 days, which must be full-time before a paid pilot or fundraise, and which missing role is most likely to stop progress.

Clarify PI and student roles, time commitment, IP source, data rights, school approval, conflict-of-interest boundaries, customer-delivery responsibility, and governance risk.

## 8. Benchmark and build-vs-buy

For each opportunity, compare:

- Foreign and domestic direct products.
- Adjacent platform features.
- Open-source substitutes.
- Service, consulting, testing, certification, and integrator substitutes.
- Customer self-build.
- True or temporary whitespace.

Mark directness as `direct product`, `adjacent product`, `platform feature`, `open source`, `service-only`, or `temporarily sparse`.

Expect customers to self-build proprietary business rules, internal portals, sensitive deployment, and domain-specific operational decisions. Expect them to buy specialized science/engineering components, continuously updated data or calibration, neutral audits/certification, hard instruments/devices, and workflow tools that remove expert bottlenecks without replacing core systems.

Browse and cite current benchmark facts. Do not list papers, generic cloud providers, or open-source projects as commercial companies.

## 9. Next-step experiments

For the Lead Wedge, define:

1. **30 days**: whom to interview or audit, what artifact to produce, and what evidence would raise or lower the thesis.
2. **60 days**: what MVP, sample, benchmark, demo, pilot protocol, or validation package to build, including what it proves and does not prove.
3. **90 days**: what customer, IP, team, funding, regulatory, pilot, or transfer milestone must be reached to decide Go, Watch, or Avoid.

For each step, include owner, target, concrete output, validation question, success signal, failure signal, and dependencies.

## 10. Recommendation format

Keep the following fields in the structured analysis source. Do not render them as a 14-field dashboard or technical-transfer checklist on one slide. Distribute them across the consulting story in `presentation-report-guide.md` and use the reader-facing language in `consulting-language-guide.md`.

For each option, produce:

1. One-sentence product thesis.
2. User, buyer, budget owner, and beachhead customer.
3. Pain and current solution.
4. Research basis and what it does not prove.
5. Product form, MVP, and customer success metric.
6. Purchase reason and build-vs-buy risk.
7. Market benchmarks and defensible gap.
8. Pilot, pricing, procurement, and repeatability hypothesis.
9. Recommended commercialization vehicle.
10. Minimum team, PI role, IP, and governance.
11. Killer risks and hard blockers.
12. 30/60/90-day experiments.
13. Weighted score and `Go / Watch / Avoid` judgment.
14. Evidence basis and confidence.

Prefer one Lead Wedge plus up to two secondary options. Keep the recommendation commercially specific and technically honest.
